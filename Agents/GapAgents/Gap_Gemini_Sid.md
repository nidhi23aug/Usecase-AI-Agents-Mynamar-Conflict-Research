Based on the provided research corpus, the following three research gaps are identified for studying cropland abandonment (CA) in the conflict-affected regions of Myanmar:
1. Granular Spatial Heterogeneity and Localized Attribution
While national or state-level trends in Myanmar are often linked to broad political-economic shifts, land-use changes at finer scales—such as village tracts or a 3000-meter village radius—remain highly heterogeneous and poorly understood. In conflict zones like Kachin, Kayin, and Shan States, deforestation and abandonment patterns vary significantly based on the specific territorial jurisdictions of localized armed authorities rather than just national trends. Current research often relies on broader administrative scales that miss these localized "micro-dynamics," making it difficult to attribute abandonment to specific conflict events versus economic or environmental drivers.
2. Quantitative Integration of Socioeconomic Paradoxes
There is a notable lack of research that quantitatively couples satellite-detected land changes with the complex socioeconomic "polycrisis" currently unfolding in Myanmar. For example, researchers have identified a "paradox" where agricultural exports (rice and beans) have increased despite intense internal conflict and rural labor shortages. Most existing remote sensing studies in Myanmar remain at the qualitative analysis stage or focus solely on biophysical indicators, leaving a critical gap in understanding how demographic shifts, market access constraints, and banking disruptions intersect to drive abandonment in specific regions like Rakhine and Chin.
3. Differentiating Conflict-Driven Abandonment from Shifting Cultivation
A major technical gap persists in accurately distinguishing long-term, conflict-induced abandonment from traditional "taungya" (shifting cultivation) and managed fallow cycles. In Myanmar’s mountainous borderlands, the spectral signatures of regenerating forests on abandoned plots are strikingly similar to the successional stages of shifting cultivation patches. Current automated classification models often produce mixed results when identifying fallow lands versus abandoned plots, which can lead to significant overestimations of forest recovery or land loss. There is a specific need for frameworks that utilize high-cadence time-series (such as SAR or HLS) to model these intra-annual and multi-year trajectories more precisely.
